Analytical questions

This dashboard is designed to answer four analytical questions about pedestrian mobility in Melbourne. Each visualisation below is labelled with the question it addresses.

  1. Q1 Which Melbourne locations experience the highest pedestrian activity, and where are these hotspots located?
  2. Q2 How do pedestrian volumes vary across years, months, weekdays, and hours of the day?
  3. Q3 Which sensor locations show increasing or decreasing pedestrian movement over time?
  4. Q4 How do weekday and weekend pedestrian patterns differ across Melbourne's urban spaces?
Slicers

Activity level groups sensors into High, Medium, and Low bands derived from their own total pedestrian counts. Click any bar or map point to focus the dashboard on that location. Scroll on the map to zoom.

Why this matters: SDG 11 and Malaysia

SDG 11 calls for inclusive, safe, sustainable cities, and Target 11.2 for safe, affordable, sustainable transport. Pedestrian volume is a direct, measurable signal of how well a city supports walking.

Finding in MelbourneParallel in MalaysiaSDG 11 link
Foot traffic concentrates in a compact, walkable central grid. The Kuala Lumpur City Plan prioritises walkable, cyclist friendly districts. Compact, walkable urban form (Target 11.3).
Many sensors record low activity, marking under used streets. Several Klang Valley LRT stations are under used due to weak pedestrian access. Safe, affordable, sustainable transport (Target 11.2).
Activity peaks at commuter hours and in the city core. KL plans concentrate affordable housing around transit stations. Transit oriented, inclusive cities (Target 11.2 and 11.3).

Pedestrian counts → locate under used corridors → target walkability upgrades → shift toward sustainable transport (SDG 11).

Sources: SDG Centre Kuala Lumpur; Spatial Walkability Index studies of rail transit access in the Kuala Lumpur City Centre and Klang Valley.

Data source: City of Melbourne Pedestrian Counting System, hourly counts joined with sensor locations on Sensor_ID, via Data Victoria and Kaggle. 888,425 hourly records, 60 sensors, 2009 to 2022. Basemap: Melbourne suburb boundaries (click_that_hood, ODbL). Counts are raw tallies and are not adjusted for sensor downtime.
